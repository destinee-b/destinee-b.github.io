Introduction

For this project, I wanted to focus on something that would be easy to classify (or so I thought), and that would be distinguishable. I decided that with the different columns of the UFO Sightings dataset, that iw would be a good option to help train a model to predict whether a sighting was considered usual or unusual. I was basing some of the usual and unusual sightings based on shapes, locations, and the frequency of times and cities that they appeared. Unusual sightings were categorized by one that was not sighted in a commonly occured area or at a time that was not very common. I decided based on this, that a good classification method would be to use multiple decision trees with the random forest classifier.

Pre-Processing the Data

For the Pre-Processing steps, I did several things to clean up the data. I first did cleaning by dropping duplicates and missing values. I also did a datetime conversion to help have additional features for the model to have a better chance of prediction. I converted the datetime to now be year, month, day, hour, and so on. This helped my model learn patterns and have additional measures to predict the sightings better. I did the same thing to the Date Posted column also, breaking it up to expand the data more. Also, I added a frequency of cities column instead of it being unique columns, this helped predict which cities were common sightings and not, rather than each one individually since there were thousands of cities. I also dropped columns that weren't needed or would overlap and cause issues, such as datatime with the expanded columns.

Modeling

I used the Random Forest Classifier to model the data. I chose this model because I thought having multiple decision trees would be a good option with the different columns and would help with better predictions. I also liked how it handed multiple data types, and wasn't as static. Since this dataset had a mix of data types, that was another reason why I chose it. This algorithm works by making multiple decision trees, then each tree has a prediction, and then the final prediction is the majority vote of all the trees combined.

Evaluation

Well, in my mind the higher the accuracy the better the model, but I know the entire story can't be told only by the accuracy. My model is around 57% accurate, which to me doesn't seem that good. I tuned it and tried to change things and that was the best accuracy I could get. I believe I lack more depth and insight into the unusual sightings area, and believe it has room for improvement. The confusion matrix helped me visualize where the misclassifications occure, and i tried refining areas such as I attempted to scale, and changed the model estimators and added max_depth, etc. For the evaluations metrics I primarily looked at accuracy, precision, recall, and f1-score. I enjoyede using these metrics to help determine performance but also to help determine areas for improvement.
